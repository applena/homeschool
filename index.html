Ramblings

As a former teacher, with a masters degree in teaching, I have so many thoughts about our educational system and the merits of homeschooing

    • “It is so annoying to have to wait for all the other kids to catch up,” my daughter’s friend tossed out in the middle of their conversation. “School is so boring!”

      I could feel the energy in my child shift. See the fire in her eyes. This was not the first time my daughter and I had encountered this attitude towards education and it wouldn't be the last.

      Coming from the perspective of a classroom teacher with a masters in education, I can confidently say that the idea of a “gifted” class of students is one of the most detrimental ideas that has permeated our educational system.

      Let’s set aside how broken the system is for identifying “gifted” students. How affluent white families are much more likely to have the time and resources to advocate for their child to be tested. How the test for a “gifted” student is nothing more than an IQ test which is deeply flawed in itself. Let’s assume that we are somehow able to magically identify all the “gifted” students and label them as such.

      What then? At best, we have identified a group of students who pick up on certain concepts quickly. It doesn’t mean that they already know the material being presented. They still need to learn just like everyone else.

      School is not a race. School is not a race. School is not a race.

      With the advent of standards based education, we have inadvertently turned school into a race. A goal post to reach for and exceed. We have completely lost sight of the true value of education which is to spark curiosity and critical thinking about the world around us. By saying that all students should know x, y, and z by this age, you end up with one group of students who say, “well, I knew x, y, and z last year so therefore I am smarter than you.” While another group of students think “this is hard, and everyone says that I should know it, so something must be wrong with me.” Neither of these statements are true.

      The truth is that every kid is going to learn at their own pace. I can’t tell you how many times well meaning teachers and administrators have tried to push my child ahead when she simply wasn’t ready for it. I don’t need my child to skim through fractions, decimals, place value - the principles that all math is based on - so that she can take calculus by high school. In the long run a solid understanding of the basics of math will serve her a lot better than a class in calculus which she will probably forget in a year anyway. Plus, if she takes the time to really understand what is happening with all those numbers, she is much more likely to find joy in that process later than if she is rushed through each class in a crazy bid to “keep up”.

      On the flip side, for those students that are “so bored”, what a brilliant opportunity to dive deep into the subject. Really spark their curiosity about how these simple concepts work. One of my favorite examples of this is done by a woman named Vi Heart and I highly recommend you watch her videos.

      By removing the goal post of “how fast can I do this” and replacing it with “how deeply can I explore this” we change the whole goal of education from a “race to finish” to a chance to find something that sparks your interest and explore it more deeply.

      I was always at the top of my class. I remember being proud that I not only had I completed calculus, but I was tutoring other students in the subject. English, science - all very similar stories. When I graduated college and started looking for a job, being at the top of my class, finishing first in everything mattered exactly zero. I no longer had someone telling me what to do, pushing me to take the next step. In the real world, the skills I needed were the very skills that had been trained out of me during my educational years.

      Working together with my coworkers Waiting to move forward on something until everyone understood Diving deep into one area rather than racing ahead to cover as many areas as possible

      What a disservice we are doing to our gifted children to teach them that the road to success is to breeze through things, covering as much ground as possible while leaving others behind. This skill set will not serve them in the real world. That feeling of superiority, owning that label of “gifted” can only hurt them with their relationship building to the other “non-gifted” coworkers they now find themselves surrounded with.

      My daughter struggles with math and numbers. However, the biggest hurdle that she has to overcome is the constant reminder that she is not doing math at the same level as her peers. Not the challenge of solving a problem. Not even the way numbers seem to move around in her head. The biggest challenge is that she compares herself to others her age. Despite our constant conversations and my encouragement, she feels deep shame that she is not “good enough”. The fact that she is not at this imaginary goal post is actively getting in the way of her learning about and enjoying numbers. Think about that. The very way in which we measure success is actively interfering with that success.

      I homeschool my child so that she can take the time and space to dive into concepts that she wants to know more about. To take her time exploring new ideas without the fear that her peers are looking down on her. I honestly believe that every child should have this opportunity but until we do a drastic overhaul of our school system and remove these harmful labels, I have very little hope that things will change.

    • When my daughter was born, I read every book I could get my hands on. I was determined to be the most amazing mother ever. This precious small human deserved nothing less.

      Aside from the obvious books like 'What to expect...', I read books on attachment parenting, crying it out, socializing your child, bonding with your child, the benefits of breastfeeding, the benefits of bottle feeding. Until one day, when my daughter was about one, I couldn't take it anymore. I threw the current book I was reading across the bathroom and cried.

      I know, that seems a little dramatic, but I was so determined to be the best mother possible and for every book I read there was another book telling me to do the complete opposite. I looked at my precious daughter and felt that beautiful rush of hormones that I can only describe as pure love.

      That was my first step. If the only thing that I could trust was that I loved this tiny human more deeply than anything I had ever felt before, I would start with that. I threw out all my books - all my parenting magazines - and leaned into what I thought I should do. It turned out that making that decision was the hardest step. Once I decided to just love on this child, everything else seemed obvious.

      I won't get into the details of how we raised our daughter when she was an infant. There are so many ways to care for an infant that my choices at that stage were never questioned, and I was pretty much doing what those around me were doing. It was when my daughter hit kindergarten that things got interesting.

      At that stage of life, my daughter was called, "spirited", "strong-willed", "defiant", "bossy", "a leader" and one professional even tried to label her with "oppositional defiant disorder". So, needless to say, her early years were no walk in the park. I could have read books about raising this sort of child - I know there are a lot of books out there to choose from - but I hung onto parenting from this place of love. It seemed so simple, and I was beginning to trust myself. I encouraged my daughter to test her limits - to see how high she could climb, to speak to kids and adults alike.

      We didn't believe in punishment. When my daughter did something like push another kid, we would talk it out. Dive into how she was feeling and what the other kid might be feeling. My mother had taught me Nonviolent-Communication growing up and I used this skill all the time raising my child. Her feelings mattered. We treated her as a human being. Someone to be respected. This did not go over well with other parents.

      My kid had a best friend in kindergarten who she adored. One day, out of the blue, her mom called me and told me that she didn't want her child around my daughter anymore. That my daughter was a bad influence. This was not the only phone call like that that I would receive over the years, but it was the first. Other parents hated that I didn't punish my child. They thought I was too permissive, that my daughter was too wild. Despite the loneliness and isolation this caused, I continued to parent the only way that I knew how - from my heart - loving and supporting this beautiful human.

      Something magical happened when my daughter hit her teen years. All of that spirit and will that we had nurtured rather than respressed transformed her into a person who knew who she was. While her friends struggled to find themselves, my daughter slipped easily into her skin. She was still the strong-willed human that she had always been but now she used that power to stand up for people who were being picked on or marginalized. She came out as gay and fiercely protected those around her who were being bullied.

      She had no trouble speaking with adults and by the time she was 15, she had three separate businesses running. One of the most astounding things about my daughter, though, is the way she feels about me and my husband. While her friends shun their parents and actively rebel, our daughter seeks us out. She speaks with us about big things and little things. She wants to spend time with us.

      Treating her with respect has become even more important in her teen years. Many would still call us permissive, but really I just see it as trusting our child. When she wants to do something, I see it as my role to support her rather than putting arbitrary limits on her. When something isn't working in our family, we all sit down together and brainstorm ways to make it work for everyone. No one is allowed to leave a family meeting until everyone is happy.

      These days, I am told regularly that I am an amazing mother. People want their children to be friends with my daughter because she is such a positive influence. I don't think that I did anything special besides trusting my child. In a society where we are taught that we know what is best and must enforce our will on our children, trusting my daughter was radical.

      There was one area where I began to question everything: education. I have a masters in teaching. I know all the best teaching practices, but while my daughter sat in a public classroom doodling on her arm, being taught by some amazing professionals, I couldn't help but feel something was wrong.

      Pulling her out of school was terrifying. I knew that I couldn't teach her. I valued our relationship too much to put myself in that kind of authoritarian position so I signed her up for online classes. She thrived. She loved the small classes online and the one-on-one interaction that they provided but something was still off. My daughter was trying so hard to participate in some classes - like ELA - while other classes like her horse veterinary class came easily.

      Listening in on her classes, I realized that much of what was being taught was actually pretty pointless. I would have a hard time staying actively engaged as well. These were teachers who were outstanding at what they did teaching to a class of two or three students. This was the best possible option. What was feeling off.

      It all comes down to trust.

      When my daughter tells me that she doesn't really enjoy ELA, I have to trust her. Reading is such a joy, but talking about a book for hours was killing her joy. What choice do I have? She has to take an ELA class, right?

      The choice has become more and more clear. Our current system of education doesn't work. If I truly trust my child, and I do, our only option is to throw this whole system of education out the window and really spend time figuring out what makes her tick. Dive deep into those areas and stop wasting time on the rest of it. The thing is - she isn't going to remember the theme from the book that she was forced to read or the quadratic function that she had to memorize for a test, but she does remember every bone in the horse's body and all the different ways that horses can get colic.

      I am once again embarking on a lightly treaded parenting footpath rather than staying the course on a highway of known ideas. I am once again questioned and whispered about at parties, but I only really know one thing and that one thing is that I love and trust my kid more than anything.
